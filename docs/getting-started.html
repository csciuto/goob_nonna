Getting Started

You don't need to know anything about synthesizers to start making sounds. This guide gives you enough to experiment — the best way to learn is to twist knobs and listen.

The Big Idea

A synthesizer generates a sound, shapes it, and controls when you hear it. Goob Nonna does this in a chain:

Oscillators → Mixer → Filter → Amplifier → Reverb → Speakers

Each link in the chain does one job. That's it. Everything else is just controlling how each link does its job.

The Modules

Oscillators: Where Sound Begins

Oscillators produce raw tones. You have two (OSC 1 and OSC 2), and each can produce four waveforms. The waveform you pick is your starting material — everything downstream sculpts it.

WaveformCharacterGood For
SawtoothBright, buzzy, rich in harmonicsBasses, leads, brass, strings — the workhorse
SquareHollow, woody, odd harmonics onlyClarinets, retro leads, sub basses
TriangleSoft, pure, almost sinusoidalFlutes, sub basses, gentle pads
Narrow PulseThin, nasal, reedyOboe-like tones, plucky sounds

Octave (the footage setting: 32', 16', 8', 4', 2') shifts pitch by whole octaves. 8' is "normal." Lower numbers = higher pitch.

Frequency detunes the oscillator by semitones. Set OSC 2 slightly off from OSC 1 (try ±0.1 to ±0.3) to get a thick, chorused sound. This is one of the simplest and most satisfying things you can do.

Mixer: Blending Sources

Three volume knobs — one per source (OSC 1, OSC 2, Noise). Turn them up to hear them, down to mute them. Noise is useful for adding breath, texture, or making percussion.

Filter: Sculpting the Tone

The filter removes frequencies from the sound. Think of it as a tone control with superpowers.

  • Cutoff — the most important knob on the synth. Sweep it and listen. It controls which frequencies pass through. Low = dark and muffled. High = bright and open.
  • Resonance — boosts frequencies right at the cutoff point. Low values add subtle emphasis. High values create a sharp, whistling peak. At maximum, the filter self-oscillates and becomes its own sound source.
  • ENV AMT — how much the envelope (see below) moves the cutoff. Positive values open the filter on each note, then close it. Negative values do the reverse. This is how you get that classic "wah" on each keypress.
  • KB TRACK — makes the filter cutoff follow the keyboard. At 1:1, higher notes sound brighter (like a real instrument). At 0, every note has the same brightness regardless of pitch.

Envelope: Shaping Notes Over Time

The envelope controls how a note evolves from the moment you press a key to after you release it. Four sliders:

/\ / \___________ / \ \ / \ \ A D S R
  • Attack — how fast the sound reaches full volume (0 = instant snap, high = slow swell)
  • Decay — how fast it drops from the peak to the sustain level
  • Sustain — the level held while you keep the key pressed (this is a level, not a time)
  • Release — how long the sound fades after you let go

Quick recipes:

  • Pluck/percussion: fast A, fast D, zero S, short R
  • Pad/strings: slow A, medium D, high S, long R
  • Organ: zero A, zero D, full S, zero R

The envelope also modulates the filter (via ENV AMT), which is where most of the expressive character comes from.

VCA: The Volume Gate

The VCA controls whether you hear anything. Three modes:

  • ENV — the envelope controls volume. This is what you want 95% of the time.
  • KB RLS — instant attack, but uses the envelope's release time when you let go. Good for organ-like sounds with a fade-out.
  • DRONE — always on. Sound plays continuously. Useful with the sequencer or for ambient textures.

LFO: Automatic Movement

The LFO (Low Frequency Oscillator) is too slow to hear as a tone — instead it wobbles other things. The Mod Wheel controls how much it affects:

  • PITCH AMT — vibrato (wobbling pitch). A little goes a long way.
  • CUTOFF AMT — wah-wah effect (wobbling the filter). Classic.
  • PW AMT — pulse width modulation (wobbling the shape of pulse waves). Gives a swirling, animated quality.

Try different LFO waveforms: Triangle for smooth wobble, Square for trills, S/H (Sample & Hold) for random robotic steps.

Reverb

One knob: MIX. Turn it up for space, down for dry. Simple.

Interesting Default Interactions

These things happen automatically without patching any cables:

  1. Envelope → Filter + VCA — Every keypress opens the filter and controls volume simultaneously. This is why the synth sounds musical out of the box. Crank ENV AMT to hear the filter sweep.
  2. Two detuned oscillators — Turn up OSC 2's level, set both to sawtooth, and detune OSC 2 slightly. Instant thick analog sound. This is the foundation of countless classic synth patches.
  3. Resonance + Envelope — High resonance + moderate ENV AMT creates an aggressive, squelchy attack on each note. The "acid" sound.
  4. LFO + Mod Wheel — The mod wheel scales all three LFO destinations at once. So you can set up pitch vibrato, filter wobble, and PWM, then bring them all in gradually with the wheel.
  5. Keyboard Tracking + Filter — With KB TRACK at 1:1, the filter opens as you play higher notes. Without it, high notes sound dull. This is subtle but crucial for playable sounds.

Patching: The Modular Part

The jacks on the panel let you rewire the synth. There are two types:

  • Outputs (red-tinted rings) — sources of signal
  • Inputs (blue-tinted rings) — destinations that accept signal

To patch: Click an output jack, then click an input jack. A cable appears. Click the cable to remove it.

The key concept: When you plug into an input, you replace whatever was connected there by default. Pull the cable out, and the default connection comes back. This means you can't break anything permanently — just unplug.

Patch Ideas to Try

LFO S/H OUT → CUTOFF IN — Random filter cutoff on every LFO cycle. Instant robot.
NOISE OUT → FILTER IN — Replace the oscillators with noise through the filter. Wind, percussion, ocean.
ENV OUT → OSC PITCH IN — Envelope controls pitch. Notes swoop up or down on each keypress.
LFO TRI OUT → OSC PITCH IN — Continuous pitch wobble independent of the mod wheel.
KB VEL OUT → CUTOFF IN — Velocity-sensitive filter (harder playing = brighter).

The Utility Modules

  • Mult — Splits one signal into multiple copies. Patch something into one jack, get copies from the others.
  • Attenuator — Scales a signal down (or inverts it). Useful when an LFO is too strong for a destination.
  • HPF — High-pass filter. Removes low frequencies. Patch audio through it to thin things out.

First Experiment

Tip: Hold Shift and hover over any knob, switch, slider, or patch jack to see a tooltip explaining what it does. Shift also switches the keyboard labels from shortcuts to note names while held.

  1. Play some notes — click the keyboard or use your computer keyboard (Z-M bottom row, Q-P top row)
  2. Sweep the CUTOFF knob slowly from high to low and back
  3. Turn up RESONANCE to about 2-3 and sweep cutoff again
  4. Set ENV AMT to about 3, attack to 0, decay to about 3, sustain to 0 — now each note has a filter sweep
  5. Turn up OSC 2 level, detune it slightly — notice the thickening
  6. Push the MOD WHEEL up with some CUTOFF AMT — hear the wobble
  7. Try a preset from the dropdown to hear what's possible

From here, just experiment. Every knob does something audible. You can't damage anything.
